# Overview

A mountain/cabin preview should be tactile, grounded, and experience-led: firelight, trails, deck views, quiet mornings, gear/practical confidence.

## Colors

Extract from wood species, stone, forest, snow/fog, upholstery, and fireplace/lighting. A snowy modern chalet and a rustic Smoky Mountain cabin should diverge dramatically.

## Typography

Use typography with weight and warmth. Avoid cold startup minimalism unless the property is architecturally modern.

## Layout

- Lead with view/fireplace/deck/arrival depending on strongest image.
- Use denser texture and warmer surfaces than coastal villa previews.
- Practical blocks should answer gear, road/access, sleeping, hot tub, fireplace, pets, and seasons where known.
- Put the date-window mock in seasonal guest language: long weekends, holiday blocks, leaf/snow/summer patterns, minimum-stay assumptions, and arrival confidence.
- Keep price comparison copy tied to a sample stay length instead of broad savings claims; owner notes should separate nightly rate, cleaning, hot tub/pet/seasonal fees, platform/service fees, and taxes where known.
- Area guides should be route/planning based: trail or waterfall days, town/brewery/restaurant categories, grocery/arrival stops, rainy-day options, and any private-land/shared-amenity rules that require owner confirmation.

## Benchmark watchouts

The Asheville Shope Creek benchmark showed that cabin previews improve when the shared-land/amenity story is practical, not poetic. If the source mentions acreage, trails, sauna, fire pits, gardens, inconsistent cell service, or no-pet rules, surface those as guest-planning details with correction gaps rather than decorative copy.

## Do's and Don'ts

Do: honor wood, stone, and seasonality.
Don't: turn every cabin into plaid cliché or luxury black chalet.
